satara

an interview

by harry


characters:

SGT. BELSNER a police officer
CHARLIE ARTWOOD a butcher

SETTING

A police interview room. Two chairs face each other across a small table. The scene is lit by a hanging lamp, gently swaying. BELSNER and CHARLIE are seated. BELSNER wears a police uniform and writes in a small notebook with the stub of a pencil. CHARLIE looks ragged and slighlty lost, but is doing their best to answer BELSNER's questions.



BELSNER
You've seen her?

CHARLIE
Course I've seen her. Every Wednesday at Rigney's. They say it's a private function out front, but Rigney lets the regulars in anyway --

BELSNER
And that's you?

CHARLIE
What?

BELSNER
A regular.

CHARLIE
Yeah, I guess. Rigney lets me in on Wednesdays so --

BELSNER
That makes you a regular.

CHARLIE
Yeah, I guess


BEAT


CHARLIE
She's there on Wednesdays. Sits in the corner booth with the rest of 'em. A Northerner, that old bloke, and a horse girl.

BELSNER
Horse girl?

CHARLIE
One of them wilders. Smells like horse.

BELSNER
Oh, I thought you meant --

CHARLIE
What?

BELSNER
Doesn't matter. Go on.

CHARLIE
Four of 'em sit in the corner booth. Two on each side. Got a sign and everything.

BELSNER
A sign?

CHARLIE
Yeah, a sign. Says 'Handsome Cat Theatre Company'.

BELSNER
Like the one down in Southbank?

CHARLIE
Like the what?

BELSNER
The theatre company.

CHARLIE
There's a theatre company in Southbank?

BELSNER
Yeah, it's called the Handsome Cat Theatre Company.

CHARLIE
Like the sign?

BELSNER
What?

CHARLIE
Like the sign the Northerner, the old bloke, and the horse girl have? The ones who sit in the corner booth with --

BELSNER
Don't say her name.

CHARLIE
I wasn't gonna. I'm not stupid, you know. I was gonna say 'her'. I'm not stupid

BELSNER
Just taking precautions. You can't be too careful.


BEAT


BELSNER
Go on.

CHARLIE
Like I said, she comes to Rigney's every Wednesday night, sits in the corner booth with --

BELSNER
The Northerner, the old bloke, and the horse girl. And they have a sign.

CHARLIE
Yeah, like a sandwitch board. It says 'Handsome Cat Theatre Company', and underneath it says 'Tryouts Tonight'.

BELSNER
Tryouts?

CHARLIE
Yeah, Tryouts. Like for their theatre.

BELSNER [ASIDE]
Auditions.

CHARLIE
What?

BELSNER
Auditions. That's what tryouts for the theatre are called. Auditions.

CHARLIE
Auditions. Tryouts for the theatre. Auditions.


BEAT


CHARLIE
Then why does it say Tryouts?

BELSNER
What?

CHARLIE
On the sign, I mean. If tryouts for the theatre are called auditions, why does the sign say 'Tryouts Tonight'? It should say 'Auditions Tonight', if that's what tryouts for the theatre are called. What's the point of having a different name specifically for theatre tryouts if you're not going to use it?

BELSNER
Maybe they don't know the word 'audition'.

CHARLIE
You're telling me these people, who run auditions at Rigney's every Wednesday night, who probably run a theatre down in Southbank, don't know the meaning of the word 'audition'?

BELSNER
No need to get worked up. You didn't know it until I told youe

CHARLIE
Yeah, well I don't host auditions at Rigney's every Wednesday night, do I? I don't probably run a theatre down in fuckin' Southbank, do I? I've not participated in any fuckin' theatre productions, because I'm a fuckin' butcher. Now if these Handsome Cat Theatre people didn't know where to find the oysters on a chook, or how to properly slit and bleed a pig, I wouldn't be surprised. But when it comes down to basic fuckin' industry procedures, they should be up to fuckin' date with their terminology.

BELSNER
No need to get worked up

CHARLIE
Fuck you, pig. You think you can come down from Hayfair and --

BELSNER [ROARS]
I said --

BELSNER [QUIETLY]
No need to get worked up.


THEY SIT IN SILENCE


BELSNER
As you were saying. She comes in to Rigney's every Wednesday night, sits in the corner booth with the Northerner, the old man, and the horse girl. They have a sign, advertising tryouts --

CHARLIE [ASIDE]
Auditions

BELSNER
-- for their theatre company, which may or may not be in Southbank. What happens next?

CHARLIE
They do the auditions.

BELSNER
Right there at Rigney's?

CHARLIE
Right there at Rigney's. Every Wednesday night. They have a chair for people to sit in, they ask them questions, sometimes make 'em do stuff.

BELSNER
What kind of stuff?

CHARLIE
All kinds. There was a bloke last week who juggled.

BELSNER
Juggled?

CHARLIE
Yeah, juggled. Balls, knives, whatever. Donnie, I think they called him. Donnie the Fool. Dressed like a clown.

BELSNER
What?

CHARLIE
Dressed like a clown. Big shoes, bells on hat, whole nine yards.

BELSNER
Weird.

CHARLIE
Not really. You get some odd ones at Rigney's. Maddy Mad Eyes, Reuben Whispers, Bulletproof Brooks --

BELSNER
Bulletproof Brooks?

CHARLIE
Yeah.

BELSNER
Why bulletproof?

CHARLIE
She's bulletproof.

BELSNER
As in...

CHARLIE
As in bulletproof. Seen her get shot point-blank in the face, nothing. Pistols, rifles, whatever. Said she even took a cannon shot once. Bulletproof.

BELSNER
Incredible. Do you know how I could contact her? That ability could be very useful in my line of work.

CHARLIE
No can do, guv.

BELSNER
Why not?

CHARLIE
She's dead. Couple months back.


BEAT


CHARLIE
Got stabbed.

BELSNER
Shame.


BEAT


BELSNER
We're getting sidetracked. I need to know what part she plays in the tryouts.

CHARLIE
Who, Brooks?

BELSNER
No, her.

CHARLIE
Oh. She just sits there mostly. Seems to listen to what everyone's saying but --

BELSNER
Sometimes it's hard to tell.

CHARLIE
Yeah. The old bloke tends to ask her what she thinks at the end of each audition, though.

BELSNER
What does she say?

CHARLIE
Not much. Sometimes she tilts her head or waves a hand.

BELSNER
And that means something to the old man?

CHARLIE
Yeah, he'll nod too, or raise an eyebrow. And sometimes the people will smile and nod and shake hands, and sometimes they go away.

BELSNER
But she never actually speaks?

CHARLIE
Sometimes she leans over and whispers to the old man.

BELSNER
And?

CHARLIE
And what?

BELSNER
And what does she whisper?

CHARLIE
Dunno, she whispers real quiet.

BELSNER
Really.

CHARLIE
Yes.

BELSNER
No, I meant -- Nevermind. You're saying she has the final say on if auditionees are accepted?

CHARLIE
Well, she -- 'auditionees'?

BELSNER
The people who audition.

CHARLIE
Nah, that's not a word. Auditionees. Doesn't sound right. Auditionees.

BELSNER
Look, it's simple. The suffix 'ee' can be added to transitive, or more rarely, intransitive verbs to form nouns meaning a person who is the object or subject of that verb. Interview, interviewee. Examine, examinee. Audition, auditionee. Perfectly valid lexical construction.

CHARLIE
Still doesn't sound right.

BELSNER
Let me get this straight. You are a butcher who, until a minute ago, did not know the word audition. I am a well-educated police sergeant, who taught you the pronunciation and meaning of said word. You are now telling me that auditionee is not a real word, based on the evidence that it 'does not sound right'. Have I got that straight?

CHARLIE
Yes.

BELSNER [SHOUTS]
I've just about had enough of your fucking lip! I am on the tail of a dangerous criminal, and you are fucking me around. You must think you're hot shit, drip-feeding the sergeant just enough so I'll go sniffing somewhere else, but I've got news for you. I'm being the good cop right now. You just say the word, you push me one step too far, and this goes from an interview to a fucking interrogation. I know who you are, I know where you work, I know the people you drink with. I know you live on 16 Dunslough Road in that shithole you call an apartment, and that you sleep alone every night. No-one to watch out for you, no one to call for help. Who would you run to anyway? Not the police, that's for sure. I fucking own you, Charlie Artwood, and you will tell me what I need to know, or so help me gods, you'll meet the bad cop. Do I make myself fucking clear?

CHARLIE
Yes.

BELSNER
Yes what?

CHARLIE
Yes sir.

BELSNER
So, let's start from the beginning. They meet every Wednesday, at Rigney's. They've got a sign that says 'tryouts' --


LIGHTS OUT

END
